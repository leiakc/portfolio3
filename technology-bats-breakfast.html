Bats cannot see well in the dark, however they are nocturnal animals. They look for their food in the dark using echolocation; bats send out sound waves and listen for their echo with their ears. From the echo, bats can sense where something is (like a mosquito to eat), its shape, and its size. Furthermore, bats receive information from their touch-sensitive receptors on their wings which are sensitive to airflow. These tiny bumps and hair follicles contribute to bats’ strong aviation skills, like sharp turns.

The game Bat’s Breakfast demonstrates the bat’s umwelt through various ways. Firstly, the screen is mostly black – the player cannot see the insects with their plain vision, they must use echolocation to perceive the insects. The player can only see the insect when the ‘sound vibration’ comes back to the bat. A bat sound plays when echolocation is used. The bat moves smoothly (unlike the insects) which shows that the bat’s touch-sensitive receptors contribute to strong aviation skills, like sharp turns. These receptors are indicated on the bat’s wings with small (but many) dots.
